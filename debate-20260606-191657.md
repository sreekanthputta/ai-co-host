> **Predicted next move (Moss, 7ms):** Scammer will ask for last 4 of SSN to "verify."

### Why it fixes the "checklist RAG" problem

# 3. Riskiest technical part and how to de-risk it

## Risk 1: “Prediction” looks staged

If the actor follows your exact script and the UI magically predicts everything, judges may smell theater.

### De-risk

Make the playbook prediction probabilistic and branch-based, not omniscient.

Show something like:

> Likely next moves:  
> 62% request payment  
> 24% transfer to verification specialist  
> 14% ask for login/access

Then when the caller picks one, the UI says:

> Branch confirmed: request payment.

That feels real.

Also: let the actor choose between two branches live. Give them a small script card with options:

- If agent asks “are you with Google?” → evade or claim partner.
- If agent asks “what’s the fee?” → say $399 or transfer.
- If agent asks “can my manager call back?” → refuse or give callback.

The system should still work if they choose either. That makes the demo feel alive.

---

## Risk 2: Moss gets reduced to a tiny hardcoded decision tree

This is the sponsor-danger zone. If the “playbook tree” is literally `if listing then ask company_name`, Moss looks pasted in.

### De-risk

Moss should retrieve from **semantic chunks**, not deterministic branches.

Each chunk should look like:

```yaml
scam_family: google_business_profile
branch: false_suspension_threat
signals:
  - listing will be removed
  - business profile not verified
  - Google Maps listing suspended
  - voice search registration required
likely_next_branches:
  - payment_demand
  - transfer_to_specialist
  - account_access_request
best_counter_questions:
  - Are you employed by Google or a third-party company?
  - What is your legal company name?
  - What phone number can my office manager use to call you back?
evidence_targets:
  - affiliation_claim
  - caller_identity
  - callback_number
  - payment_amount
```

Then Moss retrieves based on the rolling transcript. That makes paraphrase matter.

Caller says:

> “Your Maps profile is getting delisted.”

Moss still maps that to:

> “Google Business Profile suspension threat.”

A dict lookup would be brittle. Moss earns the shot.

---

## Risk 3: Minimax / voice latency dominates, making Moss speed irrelevant

You said “400ms RAG lands too late.” Be careful. Judges may know the LLM and TTS latencies are bigger than Moss retrieval.

The stronger claim is:

> Moss runs continuously during the caller’s speech and updates branch/evidence state before Minimax needs to respond.

Do not wait until end-of-turn. Use partial transcripts.

Pipeline:

1. LiveKit receives audio.
2. ASR partial transcript streams.
3. Every partial chunk updates rolling call memory.
4. Moss retrieves:
   - playbook branch
   - evidence gap
   - prior-call memory
5. By the time caller pauses, Minimax already has the current plan.

That is where sub-10ms matters.

### De-risk

If partial ASR is too much for 20 hours, fake less:

- Process every finalized utterance.
- Keep Minimax responses short.
- Precompute retrieval during the scammer’s line.
- Display Moss latency clearly.

But architecturally, describe it as continuous branch tracking.

# ARCHITECTURE

**Moss (essential — the loop, not the lookup):** Indexed against ~25 semantic chunks covering 8 Google Business Profile scam branches and 12 evidence targets. Each chunk carries trigger phrases, paraphrases, likely next branches, counter-question candidates, and evidence targets. Two parallel retrievals run on every finalized utterance: (1) *which branch is the caller in and what's likely next, with confidence distribution*, (2) *given everything said in this call so far, what evidence is still missing and which counter-question best targets it*. Call memory is inserted as live chunks every turn, so retrieval is conditioned on this specific conversation, not a static prompt. <10ms matters because retrieval runs continuously during the caller's speech and must complete before Minimax generates Alex's next line — inside the natural turn-taking gap. A dict can't paraphrase-match. A 400ms RAG lands after the moment is gone.

**Minimax (essential — dual-objective generation under adversarial pressure):** Maintains the "Alex, busy SMB owner" persona while simultaneously executing the counter-question strategy Moss recommends. Must sound natural under evasion, recover when the caller refuses, and never sound like a lawyer cross-examination. When branch confidence is below threshold, switches to open clarifying questions instead of confident targeted ones. Smaller models break this dual constraint and either drop persona or ask robotic interrogation questions.

**LiveKit (essential — the visceral live call):** Bidirectional voice loop, streaming ASR with partial transcripts feeding Moss continuously, barge-in handling for scammer interruptions, jurisdiction-aware recording disclosure, the staged call room, and the "phone rings on stage" moment. Without LiveKit this is a transcript simulator, not a live adversarial call. The recording timestamps are also what make the evidence packet quotes attributable.

Remove any one and the demo collapses: no LiveKit = no live call, no Minimax = robotic IVR, no Moss = hardcoded checklist.
